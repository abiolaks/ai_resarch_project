

Key Pillars of the Challenge
No Provided Datasets: Part of the core challenge is data engineering. You are entirely responsible for sourcing, scraping, or generating your own dataset to train and validate your models.
Robust Evaluation Pipelines: Just like in real-world engineering, evaluation is a cornerstone of this project. You must design and implement a robust evaluation pipeline.
The Hidden Test: Be warned—simply optimising for the public leaderboard is not enough. Your final solution may be stress-tested against a hidden dataset after submission to ensure true generalisability.
💡 Tip for Success: Start thinking about your data collection strategy early. A model is only as good as the data it is trained on!


Project Abstracts: 

Latent Probing for Toxicity Detection
Most modern Large Language Models (LLMs) rely on heavy alignment guardrails to prevent harmful outputs, yet monitoring these models during inference remains computationally expensive and opaque. A promising alternative lies in leveraging the model's internal representations to identify problematic inputs before they trigger a response.
To explore this, students will implement a latent probing pipeline using Gemma to detect toxic or undesirable user behavior (e.g., requests for harmful instructions). The team will record and extract internal embeddings across different layers of the network during inference. Students will then train linear classifiers (linear probes) directly on these latent representations to map out where and how safety-critical features are encoded. Finally, the team will evaluate the accuracy and efficiency of these probes, demonstrating how shallow classifiers can effectively spot malicious intent by tapping into an LLM's existing internal representations.
Latent Probing for Mental Health Sentiment Classification
Traditional sentiment analysis tools often struggle to capture the nuance of mental health discourse, which frequently relies on subtle linguistic markers, metaphors, and context-dependent expressions. While modern Large Language Models (LLMs) possess a deep semantic understanding of these nuances, utilizing entire models for real-time mental health screening can be computationally prohibitive.
To address this, students will implement a latent probing pipeline using Gemma to classify mental health sentiment from user text. The team will record and extract internal embeddings across different layers of the model's architecture during processing. Students will then train linear classifiers (linear probes) directly on these latent representations to identify features corresponding to specific psychological states or distress signals. Finally, the team will evaluate how effectively these shallow classifiers can decode mental health sentiment from internal states, providing a computationally lightweight yet highly accurate framework for non-invasive emotional monitoring.
Bridging the Tokenization Gap in African LLMs via SuperBPE
Most major Large Language Models (LLMs) struggle with African languages due to a hidden bottleneck: tokenization. Standard tokenizers are trained mostly on English, causing them to over-fragment African words into tiny, meaningless pieces. This fragmentation artificially inflates text length, dramatically increasing computing costs and degrading performance on morphologically rich or agglutinative languages.
To solve this, your team will implement a localized tokenization pipeline trained directly on native African corpora. Your team will then develop and integrate SuperBPE [1], a novel framework that introduces a hierarchical "superword" merging strategy. By combining statistical frequency with morphological rules, this approach will preserve semantic units and group frequent multi-word phrases into unified "superwords." Finally, the team will evaluate SuperBPE across major African language families to demonstrate how it reduces text fragmentation, cuts computational overhead, and improves downstream model performance.
[1] Liu, A., Hayase, J., Hofmann, V., Oh, S., Smith, N. A., & Choi, Y. (2025). SuperBPE: Space travel for language models. arXiv preprint arXiv:2503.13423.
Domain-Specific Small Language Model for Legal Advice
This project focuses on developing a domain-specific small language model (SLM) tailored for legal advice and regulatory compliance applications. Participants will apply advanced machine learning techniques to design, train, and refine a model capable of addressing real-world legal queries, analyzing statutes, and navigating complex legal frameworks.
A central component of the project is the development of a high-quality, legally robust dataset. Participants will source, curate, and structure data from verified legal documents, case law, and statutory texts, recognizing that data integrity, confidentiality, and precision will significantly influence the model's performance and ethical alignment.
The project encourages flexibility in approach, allowing participants to make independent decisions on model architecture, fine-tuning techniques, and alignment strategies (such as RLHF ). Emphasis is placed on practical application, iterative improvement, and producing highly coherent, contextually accurate, and legally sound outputs. By the end of the project, participants will have built a functional SLM and gained hands-on experience in applying AI to complex, high-stakes, domain-specific problems within the legal sector.

Intelligent Complaint Classification Using Localized Transformer Architectures
Customer support systems are frequently overwhelmed by high volumes of unstructured text, making manual triage slow, error-prone, and costly. Traditional keyword-based routing often fails to capture the nuance, sentiment, and underlying intent of complex user grievances. This bottleneck leads to delayed response times and decreased customer satisfaction.
To solve this, your team will develop and deploy an end-to-end automated complaint classification pipeline powered by a transformer-based architecture (such as BERT or a distilled variant). Your team will first preprocess a diverse dataset of real-world consumer complaints, addressing challenges like colloquial language, spelling errors, and class imbalance. You will then fine-tune a pre-trained transformer model, leveraging its self-attention mechanism to capture long-range contextual dependencies and subtle emotional cues within the text. Finally, the team will implement a robust evaluation framework—tracking precision, recall, and F1-scores across multi-class categories—to demonstrate how deep learning can drastically reduce manual triage time and improve downstream resolution efficiency.
Optimizing RAG Document Retrieval for Agronomic Advice

Every good answer starts with finding the right document. In this challenge, you build the retrieval engine at the heart of a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system: given a smallholder farmer's question, return the most relevant documents from a knowledge base of agricultural extension material — crop diseases, pests, nutrient deficiencies, soil management, climate adaptation, and fertiliser advice.
You are scored on how well your top-5 ranked documents match expert relevance judgements (nDCG@5). A simple TF-IDF baseline is provided — your job is to beat it with smarter retrieval (BM25, dense embeddings, rerankers, or hybrid approaches).
No GPU required. The dataset is small, clean, and fully self-contained.
